The two-line summary

Both directions are loved. For different reasons.

Krystal Bloom

A premium signal, before they touch it.

33% of KB respondents reached for the word "premium." Only 1% said "cheap." The crystal motif and luxe finish read as something they hadn't seen before.

Indipop

Different, daily-wear, theirs.

32% volunteered "different" unprompted — the highest single-word lift in the study. Format experimentation lands as fresh, the colour combinations land as their own, and the whole thing reads as something that belongs in a real makeup bag.

Methodology · 1 of 3

How the conversation happened.

Monadic concept test across the Purplle store network. Each respondent saw one direction at one of three pricing conditions, and answered an open-ended six-question script. Verbatim transcripts were translated, coded for sentiment and design callouts, and aggregated.

Stores were selected to cover the right mix of ASP bands — from value-catchment outlets to premium mall stores — so we could see how each direction lands across the spread Purplle actually sells into.

The pricing methodology

Three conditions, designed to triangulate willingness to pay.

The same direction was shown at three pricing conditions. The "No Price" condition is what makes this design more powerful than a standard test — it lets shoppers anchor themselves before we anchor them.

Condition 1

No Price

Shopper sees the product range with no price information. They are then asked to suggest a price band themselves. Captures unprompted willingness to pay.

48 transcripts

Condition 2

Low Price tier

Shopper sees the lower of two test pricing tiers. Establishes a floor — does the design hold up at accessible pricing, or does it feel too cheap for what it looks like?

41 transcripts

Condition 3

High Price tier

Shopper sees the higher of two test pricing tiers. Tests the ceiling — does the design earn premium pricing, or does the price break consideration?

51 transcripts

A note on the data
This was face-to-face, in-store, BA-mediated — the warmest possible way to do this kind of testing, and also the kind of setting where Indian shoppers rarely say "no" outright. So treat the numbers as directional, not decisive. Anywhere a sample is on the thinner side, you'll see it shown in muted grey throughout.

Willingness to pay

What shoppers expected to pay.

Median expected price per SKU, across all directions. Bars in muted grey indicate samples below n=15 — those numbers are directional, not decisive.

Lipstick sits in the middle
₹750 median across 32 respondent quotes. Most shoppers (14 of 32) sat in the ₹500–699 band; only a handful reached for ₹1,000+. The category bears mid-tier pricing comfortably; premium pricing is real but earned, not assumed.
Lip balm is the ceiling
₹700 median. 63% expected ≤₹699 — sharpest price sensitivity in the range. The category resists premium pricing in either direction.

Does the High tier break expectations?

Headroom is set by the category, not the tier you show.

Whether shoppers were shown the Low tier or the High tier, their expected price for a category barely moved. What sets the ceiling is the category itself — how much shoppers think a kajal, a blush, an eyeshadow are worth.

Read this
Showing the High tier didn't depress expectations — shoppers absorbed it. Eyeshadow, blush and kajal all carry premium pricing without losing the shopper. Sample sizes per tier are small (n=3 in some Low cells), so treat the deltas as directional. The headline holds: the category sets the ceiling, the design tier doesn't break it.

What this means for launch
Anchor the price where the high-ASP shopper already sits, then price down just enough to bring the low-ASP shopper up — about 20% above where they sit today. High enough that we're not leaving money on the table at the top, low enough that we don't lose the value-catchment shopper at the bottom.

What shoppers named

Four cues, one ownable signature.

Crystal / diamond shape 16%

Independently flagged as something they hadn't seen elsewhere. The shape is the most likely candidate for an ownable visual signature across the range.

Glass / glossy finish 9%

Reads as luxe — and reads as fragile. The same cue creates the appeal and the concern. See the section below.

Shape & colour contrast qualitative

"The shape and the colour contrast is one thing that people would definitely notice." — repeated, unprompted.

Embossed / patterned surface qualitative

Pattern recognition was strong — described variously as "diamond," "crystal," "gem-like."

The fragility shadow

Small in volume. Specific. Worth listening to.

Only 3% of KB respondents raised glass or fragility concerns. But these are reactions to a photograph — not a real prototype. With a physical product in hand, this signal is likely to amplify.

Why it matters more than the number
Photo-based testing under-represents tactile concerns. Weight, fragility, applicator feel — these emerge with the product in hand. Plan for prototype testing before launch.

Where Indipop's appeal lives

Colour combinations and format variety lead.

Colour combination 51%

Highest single-theme prevalence in the IP sample. Shoppers consistently flagged colour palette as the first thing they noticed.

"Good wide range of shades" / "shades are available, so that's a better option." Especially in BB tint, where range was a positive surprise.

Format experimentation qualitative

Tubes, tic-tac sticks, push-up forms — IP design language is multi-format. Shoppers read this as "different."

The shade-loudness signal

Friction is in selection — not packaging.

Where shoppers pushed back on Indipop, it was rarely about the design language itself — it was about specific shades being too loud for daily use. A solvable, mix-related problem.

"If we take blue colour, we can't use that on a daily basis." Sagarpur, Delhi · Hindi
"There was a green-coloured shadow; we can't carry green colour daily, or loud pink colour." Sagarpur, Delhi · Hindi
"Even if you make a four-in-one palette, there should be at least one colour we can use on a daily basis." Sagarpur, Delhi · Hindi
What this is, and isn't
It is: a shade-mix concern, especially in palettes and statement shades. Shoppers want one daily-wear option in every multi-shade SKU.
It isn't: a critique of the Indipop direction itself. The packaging, the format, and the range concept are all clearing. The friction sits at the shade-curation layer.

Closing

What to hold onto.

Both directions are loved — for fundamentally different reasons. Reading them as competitors is the wrong frame. Each opens a distinct shelf-position and a distinct shopper conversation.

Krystal Bloom is loved because…

It signals luxury before they touch it.

"It looked like it had a luxury finish."
  • Premium reached for unprompted by 33%
  • Diamond / crystal motif is ownable and fresh
  • Lipstick sits comfortably at ~₹775 median in this direction
  • Headroom exists at the high-tier price point
  • Watch: 3% glass / fragility shadow likely amplifies with prototype
Indipop is loved because…

It belongs in a real makeup bag.

"Simple, daily, the kind of thing that just slides into your routine."
  • Different reached for unprompted by 32%
  • Colour combinations praised by 51%
  • Format variety (tic-tac, tube, slider) reads as fresh and low-friction
  • Daily-wear identity is an open lane competitors haven't owned
  • Watch: shade-loudness pushback in palettes — needs one safe option per SKU
Three honest caveats

What this data is — and isn't.

1
BA-mediated, in-store, face-to-face.
Indian retail face-to-face yields very few explicit "no" answers. Treat both positive and negative numbers as relative, not absolute. Real conversion will be lower than reported intent.
2
Photo, not prototype.
Shoppers reacted to images of packaging, not physical products. Tactile concerns — weight, fragility, applicator feel — are likely under-counted. The KB fragility signal is the most important one to re-test in hand.
3
Question coverage was uneven.
BAs paraphrased and switched languages mid-question. Q1, Q3, Q4 were directly answered in only 25–42% of transcripts. Thematic coding compensates by mining the full respondent text — but per-SKU samples below n=15 (flagged in muted type throughout) should be read as directional only.
In one line

Krystal Bloom signals luxury before they touch it.
Indipop belongs in a real makeup bag.
Both are loved — for reasons that don't compete.
